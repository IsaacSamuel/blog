Building an Ethos

May 12, 2021

Word vomit on building an “ethos”/ code of ethics, for the masochistic. (Fair warning: it’s very “written to a friend at 3AM”. Have mercy.)

So I always kind of hate how in discussions about fictional characters, or even in-universe, when people judge characters based on what they did in moments of extreme stress. But it’s actually a pretty common way of seeing the world. Obviously many believe in things like critical moments where we really get “tested”. They might say that true you is revealed when you are under extreme pressure, and that’s what really decides what kind of person you are.

But I guess I see that as backward. As such, the rule-based “ethos” I’m suggesting sort of focuses on the opposite. It assumes the real impact you have is the way you live your day to day. Not based on “crisis” moments, or choices you made when overwhelmed by powerful feelings. (Those are probably always messy, uncertain situations that you should probably just forgive yourself for.)

An ethos can and probably should start small, and can even seem trivial. Often they will have nothing to do with morality on the surface–the goal, more broadly, is becoming a person of your word and becoming adept at forming + breaking automatic behaviour. For instance, if you were very depressed and unable to get out of bed, you might set a goal of “I will brush my teeth every morning, for three days”. That’s a goal so easy anyone can do it, which isn’t overwhelming in the slightest, and has a clear “success condition”.

And you succeed at that? You’re a good person as far as you’re concerned. If God judges you and asks you prove your goodness, you start with “I successfully brushed my teeth for three days”. And then you continue down the list. A list you’ve added to over the years, like a mosaic, or a brick wall. Each brick is a “rule” you make for yourself, an oath you make and keep.

A code of ethics in general does not have to involve this specific brand goal-making–but I think it is pretty tied to the “ethos” model, because it removes the moralizing/concept of guilt. You treat what you would normally call “immoral behavior” as a bad habit that you need to correct, not an indictment on your worth as a person. AKA, if you try to make a rule and don’t find yourself following it, you diagnose. Where did we mess up here? Was the environment unexpected? Did you get stressed and choke? Same questions your parents might ask you if you failed a test. (If the honest answer is “I just don’t give a crap”, it was not an appropriate candidate to be a rule in the first place… indicating some conflict between what you want and what you want to want.)

But let’s be real. Most of the time you fail a test, it’s lack of adequate preparation. So you do what you do with any difficult exam. Break it down into smaller, less difficult parts. As much as you need. Since we’re focusing on measurable behaviors, habit science and CBT techniques would be useful.

This can all necessitate a lot of organizational tasks, measurement of progress and goal-setting. This makes your ethos part of your “daily” life (ideally every goal should be a daily one.) Point really being, you don’t forget about it, or lose concentration.

In theory, it’s a way to define yourself in a deliberate way as much as it is a code of ethics. It is about building something from scratch. A personal “garden” of sorts. Something you can always invest in, even if you hit rock bottom. Just pick up the toothbrush the next morning and start over.

And like we talked about, when you abstract away all the details of the philosophy and look at the motivation, it’s really just judging yourself by who you were yesterday than who you are today rather than seeking to achieve a vague ideal of moral perfection, or doing “enough”. This inward-facing approach is perhaps extra important these days where we are so small, and so aware of our smallness… we inherently self-efface by placing our sense of worth on anything external.

Especially when you don’t buy into an objective morality. Objective morality comes with a lot of issues, but without it, it becomes even more complicated to answer a very important question about yourself. “Am I a good person?”

Having a strong inherent sense of yourself + your values can be totally sufficient, and indeed seems to be for many people. However a lot of people lose parts of their identity to trauma, grief, or simply never finding that grounding in the first place. An ethos framework, or some variant on what I described, may provide a healthy methodology for building or supplementing a sense of identity, knowing ourselves on a practical / emotional level, and ultimately bringing our lifestyles more in line with our values.
